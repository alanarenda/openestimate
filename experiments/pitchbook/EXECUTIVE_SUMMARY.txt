INVESTIGATION: WHY LARGER SAMPLE PITCHBOOK BASELINES ARE LESS ACCURATE
================================================================================

QUESTION: Are outliers causing larger samples to be occasionally less accurate?

ANSWER: YES - Outliers are the primary cause.

================================================================================
KEY STATISTICS
================================================================================

Overall Performance (n=5 → n=30):
  • Mean error: 31.67 → 25.00 (21% improvement)
  • Median error: 11.33 → 9.23 (19% improvement)

BUT Individual Cases (n=5 vs n=30):
  • 41.54% of observations get WORSE with larger samples
  • 22.64% show >50% error increase
  • 3.71% show monotonic error increase across all sample sizes

================================================================================
OUTLIER IMPACT
================================================================================

Outlier Contribution to Mean Error:
  • n=5:  51.32% of mean error is due to outliers
  • n=10: 50.77% of mean error is due to outliers
  • n=20: 45.59% of mean error is due to outliers
  • n=30: 49.46% of mean error is due to outliers

Outlier Transitions (n=5 → n=30):
  • 122 observations BECOME outliers (not outlier at n=5, outlier at n=30)
  • 55 observations REMAIN outliers (outlier at both sizes)
  • 156 observations STOP being outliers (outlier at n=5, not at n=30)

Net Effect: Observations that degrade show 3x higher outlier rate at n=30 (15.64%)
compared to their rate at n=5 (4.86%)

THE MECHANISM: WHY DOES THIS HAPPEN?
================================================================================

1. HEAVY-TAILED DISTRIBUTIONS
   - Ground truth distributions have extreme outliers
   - Larger samples → higher probability of capturing extreme values
   - Single extreme value can dominate the mean

2. VULNERABILITY OF MEAN-BASED ESTIMATES
   - Baselines use sample MEAN (not median)
   - Means are highly sensitive to outliers
   - One extreme value in n=30 can shift mean dramatically

3. THE PARADOX
   - Law of Large Numbers: More samples → better estimate (TRUE on average)
   - But: More samples → higher outlier probability (TRUE per instance)
   - Result: Average improves, but specific cases degrade

4. EXAMPLE - hard_16, trial 10:
   - n=5 error:  18.46
   - n=30 error: 1479.94 (80x worse!)
   - Cause: One extreme sample in n=30 pulled mean far from ground truth

================================================================================
RECOMMENDATIONS
================================================================================

1. USE ROBUST STATISTICS
   → Replace mean with MEDIAN for baseline estimates
   → Use trimmed means (drop top/bottom 5%)
   → Use winsorized means (cap extremes at percentiles)

2. DISTRIBUTION-AWARE APPROACHES
   → Fit parametric distributions (log-normal, etc.)
   → Use maximum likelihood estimation
   → Account for heavy tails in estimation

3. OPTIMAL SAMPLE SIZE SELECTION
   → Don't assume "more is always better"
   → Consider n=20 as optimal balance for heavy-tailed distributions
   → Use cross-validation to find optimal n per variable type

4. OUTLIER DETECTION AND HANDLING
   → Identify outliers before computing baselines (IQR, z-score methods)
   → Use separate models for high-variance variables
   → Flag and review extreme predictions

5. VARIABLE-SPECIFIC STRATEGIES
   → Easy variables: safe to use larger samples (n=30)
   → Hard variables: prefer smaller samples (n=10-20) or robust statistics
   → High-variance variables: use median instead of mean

6. ENSEMBLE METHODS
   → Average predictions from multiple sample sizes
   → Weight by historical accuracy per variable
   → Use prediction intervals instead of point estimates

================================================================================
CONCLUSION
================================================================================

YES, outliers are definitively causing larger samples to be less accurate in
specific cases.

Evidence:
  ✓ 50% of mean error is attributable to outliers
  ✓ 15.64% outlier rate in degrading observations at n=30
  ✓ 122 observations become outliers when increasing from n=5 to n=30
  ✓ 8 variables consistently show degradation driven by extreme values

The Resolution:
  • POPULATION LEVEL: More data is better (law of large numbers)
  • INSTANCE LEVEL: More data increases outlier risk
  • HEAVY TAILS: Amplify the outlier effect
  • MEAN ESTIMATES: Are vulnerable to single extreme values

Practical Solution:
  Use MEDIAN or other robust statistics instead of MEAN for baseline estimates,
  especially for heavy-tailed distributions. This will maintain the benefits of
  larger samples while protecting against outlier-driven degradation.
